Build StatTerrain v0.1.0

Create a polished, portable, high-fidelity frontend prototype named StatTerrain.

Tagline: Emergency-care resources and population-health intelligence.

This project will initially be built in Replit, but Replit is not intended to be its permanent hosting environment. The completed codebase must be easy to export to GitHub and deploy on Vercel without modification or dependence on Replit-specific services.

Do not ask broad product questions before starting. Treat this specification as the source of truth.

2. Product vision

StatTerrain is a public-data mapping and evidence-brief platform designed for:

* Emergency medicine physicians
* EMS medical directors
* EMS agency leaders
* EM residents and fellows
* Rural and community emergency clinicians
* HEMS and critical-care transport clinicians
* Quality-improvement users
* Medical educators
* Regional emergency-care planners

The application should help a user understand:

* What medical facilities are nearby
* What publicly documented capabilities those facilities have
* What pharmacies, dialysis centers, nursing homes, and behavioral-health resources are nearby
* What population lives in the selected region
* What chronic-disease and vulnerability patterns exist
* What access barriers may affect emergency care
* Where each data point came from
* How to export the information into an evidence-based regional brief

The core workflow is:

Search a location → view local resources and population-health context → inspect sources → generate an evidence brief.

This is a frontend prototype using realistic synthetic demonstration data.

Its purpose is to validate:

* Interface design
* Map workflow
* Facility filtering
* Population-health overlays
* Source and confidence display
* Evidence-brief generation
* Export workflow
* Mobile usability
* GitHub portability
* Vercel portability

Do not call live public APIs in this version.

Do not build the complete production data-ingestion system yet.

Do not add real patient information.

14. Population-needs interpretation

Create concise planning-oriented cards using cautious language.

Examples:

* A relatively older population may be relevant to falls, sepsis, polypharmacy, anticoagulation-related trauma, and long-term-care transfers.
* Elevated COPD and coronary-disease estimates may be relevant to EMS respiratory and cardiac protocol review.
* Limited vehicle access may affect EMS utilization, discharge planning, pharmacy access, and outpatient follow-up.
* Limited behavioral-health resources may contribute to ED boarding and interfacility transfer burden.
* Rural geography and longer travel distances may affect access to time-sensitive specialty services.
* High disability prevalence may be relevant to evacuation planning, communication access, and community-paramedicine programs.
* Limited English proficiency may support review of interpreter access and multilingual public-health communication.

Use labels such as:

* Planning consideration
* Orientation prompt
* Local verification question

Do not present correlations as causation.

Do not provide patient-specific medical advice.

Do not use words such as guaranteed, safest, best destination, or appropriate transfer facility.
